A note before you start

Self-guided EMDR is not a substitute for working with a trained EMDR therapist, especially for complex trauma, dissociation, or active mental-health crises. Use this video for self-regulation practice and to become familiar with the bilateral-stimulation rhythm. For trauma processing, find a clinician trained in the full eight-phase EMDR protocol.

Best EMDR books

A short reading list — the most widely recommended titles for clients curious about EMDR and the science of trauma.

Foundational

The Body Keeps the Score

Bessel van der Kolk, MD

The most widely read book on trauma and the body. Covers EMDR, yoga, neurofeedback, and how traumatic memory is stored physically — written for a general audience.

Find the book →
Client guide

Getting Past Your Past

Francine Shapiro, PhD

By the founder of EMDR. A practical introduction that explains the therapy and offers self-help techniques drawn from EMDR — accessible to non-clinicians.

Find the book →
Client guide

Every Memory Deserves Respect

Michael Baldwin & Deborah Korn, PsyD

An accessible introduction co-written by a patient and a therapist, illustrated. Useful for someone deciding whether EMDR is right for them.

Find the book →
Self-help technique

Tapping In

Laurel Parnell, PhD

A step-by-step guide to using bilateral stimulation for self-regulation and resource installation — the kind of practice this video supports.

Find the book →
Trauma overview

Waking the Tiger

Peter A. Levine, PhD

The foundational text on Somatic Experiencing — a complementary body-based approach. Often read alongside van der Kolk for the somatic angle on trauma.

Find the book →
Clinician reference

EMDR: Basic Principles, Protocols, and Procedures (3rd ed.)

Francine Shapiro, PhD

The standard professional textbook of EMDR therapy. Required reading for clinicians; useful for clients who want to understand the full eight-phase protocol in depth.
